Drama Techniques
Panagiotis Domvros, English and Drama Teacher, All Rights Reserved • Dark mode
Framing Script
“Drama dissolves barriers, includes all learners, and turns communication into a safe, shared experiment. It’s perfect for mixed‑ability classes and language learning.”
Legend & Age Bands
U = All ages E = Elementary School J = Junior High School S = Senior High School
Universal (All Ages)

U It’s anything but… (Prop it out)

Use an object of a manageable size and tell students that they have to imagine that it is anything but what it is. Call one student and ask them to interact with it as if it were whatever they choose it to be. The rest of the class try to guess what the “new” object is.

U The Shape‑Shifting Chair

Place a chair centre‑stage. Students, in turn, sit and interact with the chair according to its imagined type and location (throne, cinema seat in a horror movie, roller‑coaster, courtroom witness stand). They then give a line without revealing the chair type to help classmates guess.

U The Object of Creation

Divide into groups of 4–6. Announce an object or animal. Groups create a frozen tableau to depict it (e.g., a camel with four students as legs/humps and a fifth as neck/head).

U Yes, And…!

Demonstrate a simple opening line. Students create a conversation where every new line starts with “Yes, and…” and escalates the scene. Coach pace and acceptance; call “cut” at a strong moment, then debrief acceptance and collaboration.

  • Opening: “The river is full of fish.” → “Yes, and one of them is enormous.” → …

U Environments

Lead player calls a place (e.g., the beach). Stage players silently create the environment in seconds—becoming objects, characters, weather, etc. Hold the freeze for group observation.

  • Examples: beach, busy playground, school cafeteria, public library, grocery store.

U Lead with Different Parts of the Body

Class walks in a circle. Prompt them to “lead” with different body parts (nose, chin, forehead, toes, knees, chest, stomach, pelvis, arms), returning to normal between prompts. Debrief sensations and character types that fit each lead.

U A Great Wind Blows

Arrange chairs in a circle. Call: “A great wind blows for everyone who loves to sing.” Those to whom it applies change seats as one chair is removed; one player remains in the middle and leaves the game.

U Conscience Alley

Create two facing lines; a character walks between them hearing whispered advice. At the end, the character makes an informed decision. Provide prompts to keep suggestions focused.

Prompt bank (Conditionals) — opens list
  • You did poorly at your test!
  • What you should have done could be:
  • Study more carefully.
  • Not stay up late watching “Stranger Things.”
  • Pay attention in class.
  • Not talk to your desk-mate all the time.
  • Avoid daydreaming during the test.
  • Ask for clarification of difficult questions.
  • Copy from the guy sitting next to you. He knew nothing!
  • Ask your older brother to tutor you.
  • Start studying earlier in the week.
  • Not spend the whole evening texting with your friends.
  • Anything else you might think of!

U Name and Action

Circle naming game. Each student says their name with a matching action; class repeats. Harder version: cumulative memory round where each person repeats all previous names + actions before adding their own.

U Memory

Show a tray of ~20 items (or flashcards with words) for one minute. Hide the tray; students list as many items as they recall. With young learners, take turns naming items aloud.

U Mine Field

Set up obstacles (e.g., chairs, books). Blindfold one player at one end; guides at the other end call verbal instructions to navigate safely. Ideal for practising giving/understanding instructions.

Elementary (E)

E Yes, Let’s

Players scatter. One student calls an activity; the group shouts “Yes, let’s!” and mimes it until a new call. Encourage equitable turns. Variation: play in a circle giving each student one turn.

Junior & Senior High (J/S)

J/S The Lie Detector Game (Two Truths and a Lie)

In turn, each student shares three “facts”, one of which is a lie. Class guesses which is untrue. Encourage unusual but appropriate facts (travel, hobbies, encounters) for challenge and fun.

J/S Get Into Character / Hot Seat

One student in the “hot seat” takes a character and situation; the circle asks questions to build depth (feelings, choices, motives). Example: “You are Juliet the night before marrying Paris.”

J/S Characterization in Literature

Pairs: interviewer & interviewee (as a literary character). Aim for voice, mannerisms, attitudes. Present to class; invite unscripted questions.

Variation: Write a monologue from a character’s perspective, adopting voice and style to reveal inner thoughts implied in the text.

J/S Continuing Emotions

Two players act a neutral scene (e.g., returning an item). Replay the scene with secret emotion prompts per player (e.g., customer fearful, clerk jubilant). Audience identifies emotions—great for practising adjectives.

  • Emotion examples: Fearful, Bored, Excited, Depressed, Anxious, Hysterical
  • Situation examples: Doctor & patient; Teacher & student; Talk‑show host & celebrity; Mechanic & car owner; Detective & suspect

J/S Wink Murder

Players sit in a circle; secretly select a “murderer.” Transform the space into a party: mingle and make small‑talk. If the murderer winks at you, wait ten seconds, then perform a dramatic “death.” Players can accuse—wrong guesses sit out.

J/S Professions / Jobs / Character Traits

Quick warm‑up that pairs well with Encounters (Role Play). Stick a role on each student’s back. Students ask yes/no questions to discover the role, then improvise one line in character.

How to run (quick)
  1. Assign roles (use the full deck in Encounters (Role Play) below).
  2. One question per turn; polite phrasing only.
  3. When a student guesses, they join another student to improvise a 20‑second mini‑scene.

J/S The Good News and the Bad News

Circle story‑build: each line starts with “The good news is…” or “The bad news is…”. The aim is to continue the story rather than cancel the previous contribution.

J/S Conflicting Text

Two students get a situation/relationship card but must interact using an irrelevant textbook dialogue. From tone and subtext, the class guesses the relationship.

Situation Cards — opens list
  • You are two friends who have had a serious argument.
  • You are mother/father and son/daughter. The son/daughter tries to convince mother/father to let him/her stay out late.
  • You are a shy employee who is trying to ask his/her boss to let him leave work early today.
Senior High (S)

S Body Language Broadcast

Students pose to show a feeling; classmates write physical descriptions and create a scenario explaining why. Example: “Hands on hips, eye‑roll, smirk…” → infer emotion; craft context.

S Divulging Dialogue

Create character cards (brief bio + traits). Deal two to each student/group. Write a dialogue between the two characters that reveals feelings and suggests causes—without overtly stating them.

S Park Bench

Three players, each a famous/known character, start a scene on a “park bench.” The goal is to deduce others’ identities via hints (no names). Once identified, a player exits dramatically and is replaced by a new player.

S Encounters (Role Play)

Distribute profession/trait role cards to each student or pair. In groups, students reveal identities to teammates (not the audience) and script a short scene where identities are shown through behaviour, vocabulary, and subtext—not labels. Perform; the audience guesses each role.

Steps

  1. Deal one role card per student/pair. Students may add 2–3 role vocabulary items.
  2. Groups storyboard a 60–90 second scene where roles are revealed indirectly.
  3. Perform; audience notes clues and guesses roles.
  4. Debrief: Which words, gestures, or props were the best clues?

Language targets

  • Inferring from context; functional language (requests, offers, obligations)
  • Pronunciation (intonation for attitude); lexical sets by profession

Adjust or remove any roles that don’t fit your school context or safeguarding policy.
